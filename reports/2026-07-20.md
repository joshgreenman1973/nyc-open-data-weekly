## What's interesting this week

**1. A 101-degree heat wave drained the Bronx's water pressure, and the housing data recorded it building by building.** On July 2 and 3 the Bronx hit 101.5°F and 101.9°F. Fire hydrant emergency reports to 311 in the borough spiked from 115 on June 29 to 2,001 on July 2. Simultaneously, HPD logged 1,379 immediate-emergency plumbing complaints that week against a typical 245 — and 1,276 of them carried the problem code "NO WATER," across 609 distinct buildings, 1,098 of them in the Bronx. Open hydrants dropped system pressure and tenants in upper-floor apartments lost water entirely. One address, 1600 Grand Avenue in Morris Heights, generated 83 separate complaints. This is the clearest instance in the data of a heat event converting directly into a habitability failure, and because this scan was dormant it went unreported at the time.

**2. Every vacate order this week traced to fire or illegal occupancy — nothing else.** Ten new orders were issued July 13–18. Seven cite fire damage, three cite illegal occupancy, and no other cause appears at all. The largest displacement was 85-44 165 Street in Jamaica Hills-Briarwood, an entire-building order covering 15 units. Two orders landed on the same Queens building on the same day: 109-57 Liverpool Street in South Jamaica drew both an entire-building fire order and a partial illegal-occupancy order on July 14, suggesting inspectors found an unlawful conversion while responding to the fire.

**3. One Manhattan filing accounts for more declared construction value than the next six combined.** The conversion of 592 7th Avenue from commercial to residential was filed at $126.4 million. The second-largest filing this week, mechanical work at NYU's 333 East 38th Street, is $38 million. Office-to-residential conversion continues to be where the largest single dollar figures in the filing data show up, and Manhattan took 1,131 of 3,162 filings citywide — 36% of filings in a borough with about 19% of the city's population.

**4. The Bronx leads housing complaints while trailing in construction filings.** The Bronx filed the most HPD complaints of any borough this week (4,203, just ahead of Brooklyn's 4,103) while producing only 351 Department of Buildings filings — fewer than any borough except Staten Island. Manhattan inverts the ratio precisely: 2,810 complaints against 1,131 filings. The borough with the most deteriorating housing is attracting the least investment in fixing it.

**5. Rodents and pests drove a third of this week's restaurant closures.** Of nine establishments closed by the health department, three were shut for vermin or harborage conditions — MLB &Q Burger on Main Street in Flushing, Nabanno on 73rd Street in Jackson Heights and Bagel Express III on 3rd Avenue in Manhattan. Three more were closed for temperature control failures on cold holding, the classic mid-summer violation.

## Data notes

This week produced an unusually heavy crop of structural problems, most of them discovered because the scan had been dormant since March.

- **HPD complaints changed schema.** Column names are now underscored: `received_date` (was `receiveddate`) and `major_category` (was `majorcategory`). The old names return hard errors. The scan configuration referenced the old names and would have failed silently on this dataset. Corrected.
- **Five datasets have stopped publishing** at varying depths: shootings (16 weeks), crashes (5 weeks), film permits (13 weeks), DCWP inspections (8 weeks), stalled construction sites (19 months), COVID-19 counts (9 months). Six of the twelve tracked datasets are stale. This is the single most important finding for the reliability of this product.
- **`initial_cost` in DOB filings is a text field.** Numeric comparison and ordering require an explicit `::number` cast or results are silently wrong.
- **Restaurant inspection data lags roughly three days.** Any same-week pull understates inspections, critical violations and closures. Prior weeks in the trends file are complete; the current week is not.
- **`hpd_open_pct` is not a valid time series.** It measures the age of the complaint cohort at pull time, not agency performance. Flagged in the trends file for future removal or redefinition.
- **The vacate orders dataset contains a future date.** The maximum `vacate_effective_date` is August 20, 2040 — a data entry error in the source, not a projection.
- **The discovery query is truncated.** A 200-record limit sorted by update time now covers only about three days; the true weekly count of updated datasets exceeds 200 and cannot be measured without paginating. The agency breakdown above is therefore a sample, not a census.
- **Trends file edited.** March's false zeros (shootings, DOB filings) and its incorrect active-vacate-order count of 12 were blanked. Four weeks were backfilled.
- **Methodology deviation.** API calls were made with `curl` rather than WebFetch, because WebFetch summarizes responses through a language model and cannot be relied on to return exact aggregate counts. All figures here are read directly from the JSON. Temperature data is from the Open-Meteo historical archive at the Bronx centroid.

## Editorial flags

1. **The city stopped publishing shooting data four months ago.** NYPD's shootings dataset has no incident after March 31 and was last touched April 28. Crashes stopped five weeks ago. *Question:* is this a technical failure, a staffing loss, or a policy decision, and who noticed? *Next step:* call the Office of Technology and Innovation open data team and NYPD's public information office; file a FOIL request for the publishing schedule and any internal tickets on both datasets since March.

2. **A heat wave took water from 609 Bronx buildings and nobody counted it.** The July 2–4 pressure collapse is fully legible in three independent datasets but generated no consolidated public accounting. *Question:* how many people lost water, for how long, and did the city's hydrant-locking program reach the affected blocks? *Next step:* cross-reference the 609 building identifiers against DEP hydrant lock installations; contact the management of 1600 Grand Avenue, which alone logged 83 complaints. **→ Pursued; see the follow-up section below.**

3. **The Bronx has the most housing complaints and nearly the fewest construction filings.** *Question:* is this a financing gap, an ownership pattern, or a consequence of rent-regulated stock that cannot support capital work? *Next step:* join HPD complaint counts to DOB filings at the block level for the past year and identify the buildings with high complaint volume and zero filings — those are the disinvestment candidates.

4. **592 7th Avenue is a $126 million office-to-residential conversion.** *Question:* how many units, at what affordability, and is it using the 467-m tax incentive? *Next step:* pull the full job filing and any associated Housing Preservation and Development regulatory agreement; the filing declares no dwelling unit count in the summary field.

5. **Six of twelve datasets this scan tracks are stale, and the scan itself was silent for 19 weeks.** *Question:* how much of the city's open data catalogue is nominally live but actually frozen? *Next step:* run the discovery API across all 2,396 official datasets, compare the promised update cadence in each dataset's metadata against its actual last-update timestamp, and publish the decay list. That is a story in itself.

## Follow-up: the Bronx water outage, July 2–4

Editorial flag 2 was pursued. Findings below are drawn from the HPD complaints dataset (`ygpa-z7cr`), filtered to immediate-emergency complaints with problem code NO WATER, June 29 through July 7.

### The arc

| Date | Complaints | Buildings |
|------|-----------|-----------|
| June 29 | 20 | 15 |
| June 30 | 23 | 19 |
| July 1 | 72 | 52 |
| **July 2** | **522** | **310** |
| July 3 | 314 | 164 |
| July 4 | 225 | 113 |
| July 5 | 101 | 46 |
| July 6 | 57 | 28 |
| July 7 | 24 | 15 |

1,358 complaints across the window, against a baseline near 20 per day — a 26-fold spike peaking precisely on the 101.5°F day and decaying as the heat broke.

### Where

The event concentrated in the west and south Bronx: Mount Eden-Claremont (West) 162, University Heights (South)-Morris Heights 161, Soundview-Bruckner-Bronx River 151, Parkchester 130, Bedford Park 64, Highbridge 62. These neighborhoods hold some of the city's oldest multifamily stock, dependent on roof tanks and booster pumps that fail first when street pressure drops.

Two non-Bronx concentrations may be unrelated building-specific failures that coincided: 150 Lawrence Street in Downtown Brooklyn (31 complaints) and 92-23 168 Street in Jamaica (15).

### How the city disposed of it

| Outcome | Count | Share |
|---------|-------|-------|
| Closed on tenant or phone verification | 759 | 55.9% |
| Closed without a completed inspection | 406 | 29.9% |
| Inspected, no violation found | 145 | 10.7% |
| Violations issued | 41 | 3.0% |
| Still open | 7 | 0.5% |

Nearly a third of complaints closed with the notice that HPD "attempted to conduct an inspection... but was unable to complete the inspection." Three percent produced a violation.

**This finding carries a caveat that must travel with it.** The likely dominant explanation is benign: street pressure recovered overnight as hydrants closed, so inspectors arriving the next day found running water and nothing to cite. That reading describes a transient utility failure, not landlord neglect — and it points at a different problem, which is that the city's housing complaint apparatus has no instrument for recording a habitability failure that resolves itself within twelve hours. The alternative reading, that inspections were too slow or too few during an emergency, is not distinguishable from this data alone. Both are reportable; they are not the same story, and the disposition table above should not be presented as evidence of agency failure without the timestamp records described below.

### 1600 Grand Avenue

99 complaints from one building in University Heights-Morris Heights, the largest single concentration in the city. The first arrived July 2 at 6:26 p.m.; all were closed July 4 at 4:14 p.m. — roughly 46 hours. HPD inspected this building and issued a violation. It is the one address in the event where the record confirms a sustained failure rather than a transient dip, spanning the hottest 48 hours of the year.

### A dead end worth recording

**There is no DEP hydrant lock or spray-cap dataset on the open data portal.** The cross-reference proposed in flag 2 cannot be performed with public data. The 311 fire hydrant emergency counts are the only public proxy for hydrant activity. Obtaining installation records requires a FOIL request to DEP.

### Revised next steps

1. **FOIL to DEP** — hydrant lock and spray-cap installations by community district for 2026, plus pressure monitoring logs for Bronx service areas July 1–5. This tests whether the affected blocks had mitigation in place.
2. **FOIL to HPD** — inspection dispatch and arrival timestamps for the 406 complaints closed as unable to inspect. If inspectors arrived more than 24 hours after filing, the closure rate is a process artifact, and that is the story.
3. **Ownership and violation history for 1600 Grand Avenue** — to establish whether the 46-hour outage was street pressure or an unmaintained booster pump.
